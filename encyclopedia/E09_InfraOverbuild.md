**Bilateral alpha summary:**
- Risk side (generic-demand merchant infrastructure, regardless of sector): severity 10/10. This is the highest-severity sector-specific credit event in 40 years of Moody's project-finance default data. Mechanism: generic-merchant financing of demand-projection-driven capacity build, with no contracted off-take to absorb the demand mis-projection. Asset classes: merchant CCGT (A08 power), long-haul fiber (A18 telecom), speculative real estate (A19), other generic-merchant infrastructure.
- Survival side (contracted infrastructure regardless of sector): severity 3/10. Contracted power + regulated telecom + take-or-pay gas pipelines + utility rate-base assets held through the entire cycle. This is the empirical validation of contracted-off-take as the single most important risk differentiator in infrastructure credit.
- Critical validation: the sorting mechanism between survive-and-default was **ex-ante offtake discipline**, not the demand realization itself. Deals with contracted revenue survived generic demand mis-projection. Deals without contracted revenue failed regardless of asset quality, construction execution, or management capability.

## SECTION 4 — TRANSMISSION MECHANISM
*Full causal chain from macro trigger to deal-level impact. Every link states mechanism, asset class, timeline.*

**Primary chain — the overbuild cycle (generic structure applies to both sub-cases):**

Deregulation-or-new-technology opens a previously constrained market →
capital flows in at equity level (IPO boom, SPAC-precursor vehicles, private-equity partnerships) →
demand projections are constructed and propagated (internet traffic doubling rate, electricity demand growth post-deregulation) →
capex deployed on projected demand (plant construction, fiber laying) without contracted off-take →
bank debt + HY bond financing provides leverage layer on top of sponsor equity →
capacity commissions on generic-market bet (spark-spread, fiber-lease revenue at market) →
demand grows but not at projected rate (internet traffic grew 100-150%/yr not 3650%/yr; electricity demand grew 1-2% not 3-4%) →
new capacity + slow demand = revenue per unit of capacity collapses (spark spreads compress, fiber-lease rates collapse) →
operating cash flow fails to service debt →
distress cascades: accounting revelations, covenant breaches, rating downgrades, working-capital withdrawal →
mass bankruptcies, sector credit repricing, 5-10 year new-build moratorium →
industry consolidates around contracted-revenue survivors + post-restructuring buyers.

Timeline: 4-7 years from cycle start to peak default; 8-12 years from cycle start to stabilization; 10-15 years from cycle start to new-build resumption.

**Fiber overbuild sub-case specifics:**
Telecommunications Act 1996 opens CLEC competition → IPO-driven entry (Global Crossing, 360 Networks, Qwest, Level 3, Williams Communications, WilTel, McLeodUSA, XO Communications, Winstar) → "Internet traffic doubling every 100 days" (Worldcom-propagated; actual doubling 18-24 months) → $500B+ fiber capex 1996-2001 → by 2001 fiber utilization 2-5% of capacity laid → fiber lease rates (IRU prices) collapse 90%+ → bankruptcies cascade 360 Networks (May 01) → PG&E Natl Gas (Apr 01) → Winstar (Apr 01) → Global Crossing (Jan 02) → WilTel (Apr 02) → WorldCom (Jul 02) → Adelphia (Jun 02) → McLeodUSA → multiple CLECs → recovery 10-20% on senior bonds → bank losses $30B+ → 8-year new-build gap before cloud-DC-driven demand reopened the asset class (2010+).

**Merchant power overbuild sub-case specifics:**
FERC Order 888 (1996) + Order 2000 (1999) open merchant generation → IPP formation wave (Calpine, Mirant, NRG, Dynegy, Reliant Resources, AES, El Paso Electric Merchant, Duke Energy Merchant) + IOU merchant carve-outs → "Electricity demand +3-4%/yr from deregulation" (actual +1-2%) → ~200 GW CCGT announced; ~170 GW built 2001-2005 → gas prices rose from $2-3 to $5-6/MMBtu 2002-2004 simultaneously with spark-spread compression = double compression → spark spreads collapse from $15-25/MWh to $0-5 in many zones → operating cash flow fails → Enron accounting discovery (Dec 01) → merger attempts fail → ratings cascade: Calpine junk (2002) → NRG Chapter 11 (May 03) → Mirant Chapter 11 (Jul 03) → Calpine Chapter 11 (Dec 05, delayed by hedging + asset sales) → merchant power sector lost 60-80% of its equity value; senior debt recovery 25-45% → 5-year new-build gap before contracted gas new-build resumed (2008-2010); 10+ year gap before merchant-model new-build reopened in PJM/ERCOT (2015+).

**The offtake discipline test (applies to both sub-cases):**
For any generic-merchant infrastructure deal, the question is not whether demand will grow. Demand DID grow in both 1996-2001 cycles — internet traffic and electricity demand both expanded substantially. The question is whether demand grew FAST ENOUGH to absorb the capacity built on projected demand AT THE PRICING assumed. The answer in both cases was no — demand growth was 20-40% of projected rate, and capacity was built for projected. Contracted-revenue deals are insensitive to the rate-of-growth question because revenue is locked regardless. Generic-merchant deals bet on the rate of growth and lose when reality disappoints.

**The 2026 DC-power parallel:**
The pattern rhyme to today's DC-power build-out is structural, not coincidental. Key parallels:
- *Demand projection:* "AI training compute doubling every 6 months" is today's analog to "internet traffic doubling every 100 days." Even if directionally right, the question is whether extrapolation holds through the capital-deployment timeline.
- *Capex surge:* $200B+/yr hyperscaler capex is of similar order of magnitude to late-90s combined telecom + merchant power capex (~$200-300B/yr for each cycle separately in peak years).
- *6-counterparty concentration:* AWS / Azure / Google / Meta / Oracle / Apple parallel the late-90s telecom carrier concentration (Worldcom / AT&T / Sprint / Verizon / Qwest / Global Crossing). Fewer counterparties in both cycles, but high correlation in both.
- *Generic-merchant risk:* merchant-DC-hopefuls without hyperscaler anchors parallel the late-90s CLECs without incumbent protection, and the late-90s merchant CCGTs without contracted off-take. Fermi America as contemporary analog.
- *Edge-inference demand substitution (W4):* analogous to the 2000-era realization that traffic growth was not what was claimed. If edge-inference share rises from ~10% to ~40% over a 10-year window (our W4 projection), cloud-DC demand growth trajectory is cut meaningfully — parallel to the 2000-era demand-projection failure.
- *Infrastructure timeline-mismatch:* DCs build in 18-24 months; power + transmission take 3-10 years. Parallel to the late-90s mismatch between rapid fiber laying and slow demand ramp.

The structural test for any 2026-era DC-power deal: is this contracted-anchor or generic-merchant? The 2001-03 sort was extreme (40% CDR on merchant vs 3-4% on contracted). Nothing in the current cycle suggests the sort will be less extreme — the underlying mechanism (demand-projection-failure-meets-pre-deployed-capex) is the same.

## SECTION 5 — RESOLUTION SIGNALS
*What ended the stress. First indicators of normalization.*

**What ended the stress:**
- Bankruptcy cascade completed (most large merchant + telecom players through 2003; Calpine delayed to Dec 2005)
- Asset values cleared through secondary markets (fiber IRUs marked down 95%+; merchant CCGT trading $200-400/kW vs $600-800/kW build)
- Industry consolidation — survivors + post-restructuring buyers acquired distressed assets at $0.15-0.40/$ of book
- Demand growth continued at actual (not projected) rate — utilization gradually recovered over 5-10 years
- Rate-regulated / contracted-revenue infrastructure held continuously through the crisis
- Cross-contagion to contracted assets normalized within 12-18 months
- New-build moratorium allowed demand to catch up with overbuilt capacity

**First indicators of normalization (earliest appearance):**
1. Merchant power equity sector stabilization — mid-2003 (post-NRG/Mirant bankruptcies)
2. Telecom equity sector stabilization — Q4 2003 (post-WorldCom emergence as MCI)
3. Secondary-market asset trades clearing at stable prices — 2003-04 (buyer floor established)
4. Contracted-infrastructure spread normalization — 2003-04 (cross-contagion dissipated)
5. Private-equity merchant acquisitions at clearing prices — 2004-05 (KKR, Blackstone, TPG entering)
6. Rating agency sector outlook stabilization — 2004-05

**Recovery timeline by asset class:**
- A08 Merchant power (CCGT): no new-build 2003-2008; asset-class reopening began 2010-2012 with PPA-supported new-build; merchant new-build returned in PJM 2013+ / ERCOT 2015+
- A18 Fiber / long-haul telecom: no new-build 2002-2010; cloud-DC-driven buildout resumed 2010-2012
- Contracted thermal (A07 A14 A15): no interruption — performed continuously
- Regulated utility (A01-A04 generation): no interruption
- Incumbent telecom (ILEC): no interruption
- ILEC wireless divisions: expanded through the crisis unaffected

**What did not resolve quickly:**
- Merchant-model reputation: 5-10 year lag before merchant-risk sector credit was rebuilt; wider spread on merchant deals persisted into 2010s
- Rating-agency methodologies for merchant power and telecom infrastructure: methodology revisions embedded a permanent merchant-risk-spread vs contracted-revenue-spread differential
- Bank lending appetite for generic-merchant: multiple banks (Deutsche, UBS, Citi) permanently reduced merchant-credit desk capacity
- Sponsor reputation: several sponsor names (Dynegy, Reliant, AES merchant arm) took 10+ years to re-establish market access

**The core lesson, pending Sri validation:**
The demand projection was directionally correct in both cases. Internet traffic did grow. Electricity demand post-deregulation did grow. The overbuild was not caused by the demand projection being wrong — it was caused by capex being deployed on **projected rate** when only **realized rate** matters for debt service. Contracted off-take insulates the deal from the rate-of-growth question. Generic-merchant does not. The sort between survive-and-default in 2001-03 was determined entirely by this ex-ante discipline, not by the demand realization itself. The 40% merchant CDR vs 3% contracted CDR in Moody's data for that cohort is the single cleanest empirical validation of the contracted-off-take principle in 40 years of project-finance data.
