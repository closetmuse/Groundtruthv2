### Non-obvious observation

**Maine is not a moratorium story — it is a ratepayer-commission politics story that is mispriced.**

Classical NIMBY opposition saturates at the local level: noise, traffic, water, aesthetics. It wins a zoning board, it rarely wins a state legislature. The Maine prints, paired with the Bisnow Apr 18 panel framing (GS-991 *"Data Center Power, Land And Opposition With Tract's Graham Williams"*) and GS-786 *"High-Density AI Is Forcing a Power Reckoning at the Rack,"* reveal that the mechanism has shifted. Opposition is no longer running on aesthetics. It is running on **ratepayer-burden and grid-stress arguments**, which compound at the same institutional level where interconnect approval already lives. A PUC or legislature persuaded that AI-driven DC demand raises retail electricity rates by X% will not limit its action to moratoria — it will push rate-design and interconnect-cost-allocation changes that directly affect project underwriting.

If Maine is the first of a class rather than an idiosyncratic outlier, the next 60-90 days should see similar motion in states with (a) material AI load growth and (b) live retail-rate politics: Virginia is the obvious first candidate, Ohio and Iowa the second tier, Georgia and Texas the slower-moving third. Seven days of data is not enough to say Maine is a class, but today's Tennessee litigation suggests the *legal architecture* on both sides is organizing faster than a one-state event would predict.

**SMR paired finding:** GS-707's fact-check is the week's most important sector read. A DC development plan that treats SMR uptake as its baseload answer on a 3-year decision horizon is underwriting on a construction-feasibility timeline with no at-scale empirical basis. Vogtle is the only US construction-complete reference in thirty years, and it ran 7 years late and $17B over budget. The Apr 18 Vogtle ratepayer-pain print is not nostalgia — it is a live political signal about what happens to commissioners who bet on nuclear. Big Tech rushing into MOUs is not Big Tech getting MW on the grid.

**The silence is itself a signal.** Zero PPA prints, zero interconnect-queue prints, zero hyperscaler-capex revisions in a week where siting risk escalated and SMR execution cracks became visible. Either deal-flow is pausing for regulatory clarity, or the deals in flight are below the noise floor of our public-source feeds. Both readings are consistent with mid-cycle risk repricing rather than continued issuance.
