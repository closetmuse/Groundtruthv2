================================================================
WHY THIS IS HAPPENING NOW
================================================================

Four structural pressures converged in 2024-25 to make oil-gas-hub DC siting a real option rather than a fringe thought experiment:

1. **Tier-1 DC market grid saturation.** Northern Virginia (Loudoun/Prince William), Dallas-Fort Worth, Phoenix, Silicon Valley, Chicago — all have grid-interconnection queues stretched to 3-5+ year wait times. Dominion (NoVa), ERCOT, APS (AZ) have publicly flagged that expected large-load additions exceed planned transmission buildout. The grid can't absorb the DC demand at traditional sites.
2. **Natural gas price dispersion at hubs.** Waha basis differentials frequently trade $1-3/MMBtu below Henry Hub; Permian associated gas is functionally stranded at well-head because pipeline takeaway capacity binds. DC developers can access $1.50-2.50/MMBtu gas at Permian, vs $3-4/MMBtu delivered to traditional DC locations. Fuel cost for a captive CCGT is ~60-65% of LCOE; a $1.50/MMBtu input advantage is a 20-30% generation-cost advantage.
3. **Behind-the-meter regulatory window opening.** Under the Trump administration, FERC and DOE posture toward behind-the-meter large-load configurations has softened. The Amazon-Talen PJM nuclear-DC behind-the-meter deal (initially rejected Nov 2024, with partial relief granted subsequently) set the pattern that BTM DC is no longer automatically blocked. Gas-fired BTM DC does not raise the same retail-customer-cross-subsidization concerns that the nuclear-BTM fight raised.
4. **AI training cluster power-density economics.** Training clusters are now 100-500+ MW single-site; GPU-dense racks push power density from the 2015-era 5-10 kW/rack to 50-100+ kW/rack. Single-site multi-100-MW power buildout is easier with a captive CCGT than through grid interconnection at a tier-1 market. Campus-scale design (single large GPU cluster, not fungible multi-tenant) aligns with captive power generation.

================================================================
MECHANISM, TIMELINE, ASSET SPECIFICITY
================================================================

**Mechanism — the physical and commercial logic:**

- *Physical:* Site the DC within pipe-distance of a producing gas field or pipeline hub. Build captive CCGT on site (typically 300-600 MW, modular). Direct gas-pipeline interconnect via short lateral (few miles, ownable or contracted). No grid interconnection required for primary power; grid tie-in may exist as backup or for load balancing. Fiber may or may not exist; fiber-build cost is part of the economic calculation.
- *Commercial:* Developer typically forms a special-purpose vehicle holding both the CCGT and the DC shell. Gas supply contracted long-term (10-15 yr) from a producer or midstream counterparty. Off-take on the DC side could be hyperscaler-anchored (tier-1) or merchant with speculative build. Financing structure: project-finance senior debt on the CCGT, DC senior debt on the shell, equity at the SPV or holdco layer.
- *Regulatory:* BTM configuration means the DC is not a grid customer — avoids interconnection queue, transmission cost allocation, capacity-market dependency, and (in some structures) state retail-rate oversight. Permits required: gas pipeline lateral (FERC or state depending on jurisdiction), air permit for CCGT (state EPA), local siting. Typically faster than full grid-interconnection track.

**Timeline — the cadence of adoption:**

- **Bitcoin-mining precursor (2019-2023):** Permian flare-gas BTC mining established the technical pattern. Developers like Crusoe Energy built the early templates for stranded-gas-to-compute conversion.
- **AI pivot (2023-2025):** Crusoe-type operators re-pivoted from BTC to AI training. Crusoe raised $1.5bn at $2.8bn valuation in 2024 for AI-cluster buildout. Permian was the proving ground; the model was demonstrated at 100-300 MW scale.
- **Mainstream DC developer adoption (2025-2027):** Traditional DC developers — Digital Realty, QTS, Vantage, CyrusOne, Stack, Edged, Aligned — now exploring oil-gas-hub sites alongside tier-1 market builds. First hyperscaler-anchored announcements expected 2026. Bisnow's piece today suggests this is now pattern, not outlier.
- **Peak locational-siting cycle (2027-2030):** If the thesis holds, 15-25% of new US DC capacity adds in 2027-2030 could be at oil-gas-hub locations. Traditional tier-1 locations remain primary but grow slower.
- **Reversion or maturation (2030+):** Outcome depends on (a) AI-training-workload demand persistence — if training demand saturates, locational flexibility matters less; (b) grid buildout catch-up at tier-1 markets; (c) regulatory changes on BTM.

**Asset specificity — US pipeline-class / vehicle impact:**

- **Crusoe-type pure-play AI DC at oil-gas hubs:** First-mover category. Equity-heavy so far; senior debt structures still developing. Senior-debt pricing reference is short, spread is wide. If the model scales, senior-debt pricing tightens — creates a new financing window.
- **Hyperscaler-anchored BTM DC at oil-gas hubs:** If Microsoft, Meta, AWS, Google announce locationally-anchored BTM deals, senior debt on the shell is near-IG because of the anchor credit. Tier-1-hyperscaler-anchored financing economics apply with the added benefit of captive-power cost advantage.
- **Merchant (non-anchored) BTM DC at oil-gas hubs:** This is where **ALF-19-1 becomes directly relevant.** A merchant-DC developer at a Permian or Haynesville site has (a) lower interconnection risk, (b) captive-power cost advantage, (c) but single-site concentration risk and no hyperscaler credit anchor. Senior debt will price wide absent credit enhancement. **Candidate consumer of an ILS-wrap-to-IG structure.** In fact, merchant-oil-gas-hub DC may be the archetype consumer of ALF-19-1's wrap structure — the locational advantage compensates for the lack of hyperscaler anchor, and the wrap closes the residual credit-risk gap for IG capital-pool access.
- **Captive CCGT at oil-gas hubs (the power asset):** Project-finance senior debt on 300-600 MW CCGTs with gas-feedstock contract and DC off-take contract. If DC off-take is hyperscaler-backed, senior debt is conventional project-finance IG. If DC off-take is merchant, the CCGT senior debt's exposure flows through to the DC credit — wraps or guarantees become relevant.
- **Upstream gas producers (Permian, Haynesville, Marcellus, Bakken operators):** New long-term off-take for gas. Structurally credit-positive for producers that secure long-dated DC-anchored gas supply agreements. Relevant to E&P credit — not directly in Sri's project-finance book but adjacent.
- **Midstream gas (pipelines, processing):** If DC locations spawn new short pipeline laterals, midstream builders (Kinder Morgan, Enterprise, Enbridge, Energy Transfer, MPLX, Williams) get incremental throughput. Modest revenue add but continuous over long contract terms.
- **Tier-1 DC market operators (NoVa, DFW, Phoenix, SV, Chicago):** Not displaced but growth rate slows relative to prior expectations. Queue constraint remains binding. Valuation cap on tier-1 vs oil-gas-hub builds starts to matter.

================================================================

This third category shares some characteristics with each of the first two:
- *Like hyperscaler-anchored:* has a structural advantage (captive gas cost, faster siting) that differentiates it from undifferentiated merchant "build and they will come" speculation.

**For ALF-19-1 (ILS-wrapped DC senior debt IG uplift), locationally-anchored merchant BTM DC is the archetype demand case.** The ILS-wrap structure compensates for the lack of hyperscaler credit anchor; the locational advantage provides the underlying economic viability that makes the DC commercially defensible. If the oil-gas-hub DC siting pattern scales through 2026-2028, ALF-19-1's contract-survival test (first ILS-wrapped merchant-DC senior-debt transaction closing inside 90 days) becomes **more likely and faster-occurring** than previously modeled. CROSS-AXIS THREADING
================================================================

This is an axis-crossing observation — unusually:

- **LNG Axis 3 (Waha basis).** Captive Permian gas for DC BTM competes with LNG export demand for Waha gas. Long-dated DC-anchored gas off-take agreements reduce Waha basis pressure over time (more demand supports basis); short-term the flow is neutral. Waha basis becomes a DC-economics input, not just an LNG-economics input.
- **Power Axis 3 (Interconnection / FERC).** DC demand that bypasses grid interconnection reduces queue pressure on transmission operators. Paradoxically, BTM DC at oil-gas hubs relieves grid buildout pressure at tier-1 markets — a supply-side safety valve on the grid-interconnection crisis. FERC Large Load Docket (BE-003, 9 days away) will almost certainly reference this locational pattern.
- **Power Axis 6 (BTM).** Direct hit. Oil-gas-hub DC is the archetypal BTM at scale.
- **DC Axis 1 (Hyperscaler investment durability).** Adds a new dimension — locationally-differentiated builds. Tier-1 hyperscaler locational choices become more important than pure capacity-anchor commitments.
- **DC Axis 6 (BTM power).** Direct hit — this is the structural pattern the axis tracks.
- **DC Axis 7 (ILS-wrapped senior debt).** Demand-side accelerant — see second-order above.
