THESIS A — THE ME-OIL GEOPOLITICAL PREMIUM IS NOW STRUCTURAL
================================================================

**Claim.** The geopolitical-premium component of Brent has crossed from episodic ($5-15/bbl, transient, fades on de-escalation news) to structural ($15-25/bbl, persistent, does not fade on de-escalation news). The Brent floor that held at $50-60 from 2015-2020 (Saudi-OPEC management floor) and at $65-75 from 2021-2024 (post-pandemic capital-discipline floor) has restructured to $80-90 (geopolitical-premium-plus-shale-marginal floor) for a multi-year horizon.

**Mechanism.**

The geopolitical-premium component prices the expected-loss of crude supply from a Hormuz disruption, a Gulf-state escalation, or a sanctions-tightening event. The math is:

```
GP_premium = Σ (P[disruption_i] × Loss[disruption_i] × Duration_factor[disruption_i])
```

Pre-Iran-war (call it pre-March-2026), each factor was small:
- P[disruption] for any given week: ~0.5-1%
- Loss given disruption: 5-15% of seaborne supply for 5-10 days
- Duration factor (compounded across the curve): ~0.3 (markets assumed quick resolution)
- Product: small × small × small = $5-15/bbl premium, mostly priced into Brent vs WTI spread.

Post-Iran-war substrate (current, Day 56):
- P[disruption] for any given week: 3-5% (war is active; escalation possible at any time)
- Loss given disruption: 10-30% of seaborne supply, plausibly for 30-90 days (war means sustained, not flash, disruption)
- Duration factor: ~1.0 (markets are now pricing the sustained-disruption tail seriously)
- Product: meaningful × meaningful × meaningful = $15-25/bbl premium, **and structurally embedded in absolute Brent rather than just spread to WTI.**

**Asset specificity.**

- **Brent crude:** the floor moves to $80-90. Tests of the floor (Iran-talks-hopes weekends, OPEC+ surprise releases) become buyable rather than fadeable. Friday's intraday recovery from $99.37 midday low to $99.78 close on de-escalation tape is the cleanest day-cadence example.
- **WTI / Permian basket:** floor moves more modestly to $70-80 because US shale faces less direct geopolitical exposure but inherits the floor through Brent-WTI spread compression.
- **Brent-WTI spread:** widens structurally because the geopolitical premium attaches to Brent more than WTI. The Friday close $4.90 spread is the new normal vs the 2022-2024 $1.50-2.50 average.
- **US LNG netback:** asymmetric — feedgas costs (HH-linked) do not inherit the geopolitical premium; spot-LNG cargo prices (JKM/TTF) do partially inherit it through the residual oil-indexation in long-term contracts. **HH-JKM and HH-TTF arb gross widens structurally** as a second-order consequence of Thesis A. This is a direct read on ALF-15-4.

**Cross-asset transmission — the petrodollar reframe in plain mechanism terms.**

The petrodollar reframe (GS-2088) matters here because it removes a *demand-side* mitigant. The mechanism deserves explicit unpacking because the loss-of-mitigant is what makes the GP premium structurally durable rather than mean-reverting.

*The textbook feedback loop (what the petrodollar story said happens).*

1. Oil priced in USD globally — producers receive USD revenue when oil rises.
2. Saudi/Gulf producers (historically) recycled most of that surplus into USD-denominated assets: Treasuries, US equities, US real estate. This was the "petrodollar recycling."
3. Recycling = persistent buy-side flow on USD assets = USD strengthens.
4. Stronger USD makes USD-priced oil more expensive for non-USD-economy buyers (Europe, Japan, China, EM).
5. Those buyers cut demand → oil price falls back.

Net effect: oil rising automatically triggered an FX feedback that destroyed oil demand and pulled oil back down. That is the *demand-side mitigant* / *natural ceiling* — a stabilizer that fires without anyone choosing to fire it.

*Why the FT calls it mythology.*

- There was never a formal Saudi-USD pact. The 1974 "agreement" was about military protection in exchange for oil-priced-in-USD, but USD-pricing was already the pre-existing global convention. There was no contract requiring recycling into USD assets.
- Producer reserve diversification has been running since ~2014. Saudi USD-asset share of reserves has fallen from roughly 80% to 55-60%. PIF and ADIA deploy globally — into EUR, GBP, JPY, gold, EM private equity, not just Treasuries.
- China-Saudi RMB-settled oil deals are small in volume but symbolically meaningful — they prove the linkage isn't structural, just conventional.
- The empirical Brent/DXY correlation has weakened significantly since 2020. The recycling channel still exists but is no longer dominant.

The FT's argument isn't that recycling never happened — it's that the linkage between oil-up and USD-up has been weaker than the framework assumed for at least a decade, and we are now seeing the conventional ceiling fail to fire when it "should."

*What removing the mitigant means concretely.*

If the FX feedback no longer fires, the only ceiling left on oil is direct demand destruction — consumers paying more at the pump until they actually cut driving / heating / industrial use. That's a much slower, much less elastic mechanism than the FX channel. Slow-elasticity ceilings are higher ceilings. So oil can run further at any given supply-disruption probability, and the GP premium that builds during a crisis takes longer to mean-revert.

*The Friday tape as live confirmation.*

Textbook prediction: Brent at $99.78 should be pulling USD higher. We should see DXY pushing toward 100-102 if the petrodollar feedback were firing.

What we actually see: DXY at 98.51 — USD *softer*, not stronger. The two prices are moving in directions the textbook says they shouldn't. That divergence is the reframe playing out in real time on a single tape.

*Honest caveats (so the read can be stress-tested).*

- DXY is a basket vs EUR/JPY/GBP/CAD/CHF/SEK. Soft DXY can also reflect EUR strength (ECB-vs-Fed expectations) rather than oil-flow USD weakness. The Friday tape is suggestive, not dispositive.
- Recycling did happen historically — the loop wasn't pure mythology, just weaker than framework-priced.
- Fed cut expectations, US fiscal trajectory, and Treasury auction demand are also pulling on USD independently. You cannot attribute all of Friday's DXY softness to oil flow.

But: even with those caveats, **the absence of the textbook USD-strength response to $99.78 Brent is the data point.** A year ago that print would have lifted DXY by 0.3-0.5 intraday on the recycling-flow expectation alone. Friday it didn't. That is the empirical anchor under the FT's "mythology" framing.

Net for Thesis A: the GP premium does not have an automatic stabilizer pulling it back. The floor is more durable than the pre-reframe framework would have priced it.

**What would invalidate Thesis A.**

- US shale capex re-acceleration to pre-2020 elasticity. (Watch Permian rig count + private-operator capex disclosures Q2 2026.)
- An OPEC+ supply-shock release of 2-3 mbpd that holds Brent below $75 for 60+ days.

THESIS B — THE NON-FOSSIL STRUCTURAL FLOOR IS STRONGER THAN COVID+UKRAINE
================================================================

**Claim.** The structural floor under non-fossil energy economics (utility solar/wind PPA prices, nuclear capacity-payment levels, firm-clean DC offtake terms) is now meaningfully higher than the floors set during COVID-2020 or Ukraine-2022 — and is held by demand-side mechanisms that did not exist in either prior cycle. Even with the Trump administration's stated rollback posture on IRA and FERC, the rollback faces a demand-driven counter-current that is materially harder to overpower than legislative rollback would have been in 2017.

**Mechanism — five layers.**

**Layer 1 — AI-DC firm-clean offtake creates demand-pull that did not exist pre-2024.**

The hyperscalers (Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta, Oracle) have collectively committed 30-50 GW of clean-firm-power offtake over the 2025-30 horizon. The Microsoft-Three-Mile-Island restart (2024), the Amazon-Talen Susquehanna BTM (2024), Google-Kairos SMR (2024), Meta-multiple-utility solar PPAs (2025), and the X-energy IPO Friday (2026-04-24) are sequential public expressions of a demand wave that is bid-on regardless of subsidy. **The offtake is paying capacity-payment levels that price-clear above merchant-cost-of-energy** — meaning the firm-clean asset is selling its availability/firmness/clean-attribute, not its energy. This is structurally different from COVID-era PPAs (subsidy-led) and Ukraine-era PPAs (gas-displacement-led).

**Layer 2 — SMR / advanced-nuclear capital formation has crossed the public-equity threshold.**

X-energy +27% IPO debut Friday is one data point; Oklo, NuScale, and TerraPower are the broader cohort. Public equity availability for SMR developers means the cost of capital for SMR-anchored DC builds has compressed by 200-400 bps vs the 2022 baseline. **Compressed cost of capital for new firm-clean = structurally lower delivered $/MWh on long contracts** — which raises (not lowers) the floor under PPA pricing because the breakeven of the marginal new-build clean asset has fallen.

**Layer 3 — BTU-to-GPU vertical integration triggers parallel firm-clean build.**

Even gas-fired DC builds (Nscale-Monarch BTU-to-GPU, Wärtsilä Ohio+Texas reciprocating-engine variant — Friday corroboration) trigger parallel firm-clean offtake on the same campus because hyperscalers want carbon-attributed clean power for their 24/7-clean-energy commitments. **Every BTU-to-GPU MW built today carries a co-procurement of clean-firm MW** (typically through SMR, geothermal, long-duration storage, or contracted utility-scale solar-plus-storage). The gas build does not cannibalize clean — it pulls clean in parallel.

**Layer 4 — Geopolitical premium on fossil reinforces clean substitution.**

This is the cross-thesis interaction. If Thesis A is right and Brent floor is $80-90 with $15-25 of geopolitical premium embedded, then **fixed-price clean PPAs become more attractive even if their nominal $/MWh is higher**, because the implied volatility-cost of the fossil alternative has widened. A buyer comparing $55/MWh fixed-price solar+storage to $40/MWh merchant-CCGT-passthrough used to see the merchant option as cheaper; with $15-25 of GP premium plus the petrodollar-reframe-removal-of-USD-cap, the merchant option's expected value moves above $50/MWh and its variance moves up sharply. **Risk-adjusted, the fixed-price clean PPA is now cheaper.**

**Layer 5 — State-level and EU-level ratchet effects make rollback hard.**

The Trump-FERC rollback narrative has been weaker than the 2017 baseline anticipated. Friday-tape evidence: Maine governor REJECTS first-in-nation DC-pause bill (GS-2068 from Friday EOD; positive for DC build-out AND for the firm-clean offtake bound to it). Virginia grid-reform + community-solar landmark (GS-2058 Friday EOD). State-level pro-build sentiment has crystallized in a way that survives federal rollback. EU CBAM + REPowerEU continue to pull EU clean-build forward independently of US politics, and the EU pull on US clean-firm-export is a residual demand layer.

**Asset specificity.**

- **Utility-scale solar PPAs:** floor moves from ~$25-35/MWh (2022 IRA-equilibrium) to $40-55/MWh (2026, with AI-DC bid-on factor). Direct read on Power Axis 4 deals (CAISO-exposed solar) and on the broader US solar PPA market that shows up in Project Three Amigos / SB Energy / Aypa portfolios.
- **Battery storage:** floor moves on capacity-payment basis from ~$80-120/kW-yr to $130-180/kW-yr because the 4-hour-storage product is now bid-on by AI-DC alongside gas peakers.
- **SMR / advanced nuclear:** capacity-payment level for new-build moves to $90-130/MWh range (firm, 20-year contract) — well above merchant CCGT levelized cost but priced on firm-clean attribute.
- **Geothermal / long-duration storage:** earliest-stage floor support, but Friday's GS-2050 oil-and-gas-industry-advancing-geothermal piece is a tape signal that capital is forming.
- **Onshore wind:** less floor expansion than solar because permitting frictions remain binding; but PPA prices have firmed to $35-48/MWh range.

**What would invalidate Thesis B.**
- A 50%+ rollback of IRA tax credits (production tax credit + 45Y) AND a parallel collapse of state RPS standards in 5+ major states. (The two-leg test matters — federal-only rollback is insufficient.)
- AI-DC capex pause (hyperscaler Q1-Q3 2026 capex disclosures coming this earnings cycle is the binding next data).
- Sustained Brent below $60 for 6+ months (collapses the GP-premium reinforcement layer in Thesis A and removes the variance-cost case for fixed-price clean PPAs).
- A multi-quarter SMR-IPO failure cohort (2-3 follow-on SMR IPOs failing to debut at premium) — would invalidate Layer 2 capital-formation assumption.

CROSS-THESIS INTERACTION — WHERE THE TWO FLOORS MEET
================================================================

The two theses are not independent. They reinforce each other in three specific ways:

**Reinforcement 1 — Variance-cost of fossil widens the fixed-price-clean case.** Already covered (Thesis B Layer 4). The mechanism is: GP premium on fossil + petrodollar reframe removing USD-cap = higher expected fossil cost AND higher variance of fossil cost = better risk-adjusted economics for fixed-price clean.

**Reinforcement 2 — National-security framing of US energy supports BOTH floors.** The GS-2085 framing of "energy supremacy forged in war" treats US energy posture (fossil AND clean) as a national-security instrument. This means: (a) US shale gets capital-availability and permitting support that floors the supply-side at higher levels; (b) US firm-clean (SMR, advanced reactors, domestic battery manufacturing) gets explicit national-security framing that floors the demand-side AND the supply-side. **The Trump-administration rollback narrative becomes selectively soft on firm-clean once it's framed as national-security.** Watch for SMR-as-national-security framing in administration tape over the next 90 days.

**Reinforcement 3 — Both floors widen the gap between contracted and merchant infrastructure.** This is Sri's domain. If both energy costs (fossil and clean) have higher floors with higher variance, then the relative attractiveness of contracted-cash-flow infrastructure (long-term offtake, capacity payments, availability-based contracts) over merchant-exposed infrastructure widens materially. **Cost of capital for contracted infrastructure compresses; cost of capital for merchant compresses much less.** The structural advantage of project-finance-native structures (long-tenor, fixed-pricing, contracted-counterparty) increases.
