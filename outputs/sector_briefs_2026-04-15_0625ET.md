THE RBN ENERGY SECONDARY-MARKET ARTICLE — WHAT IT SAYS AND WHY IT MATTERS
The article ("Win, Lose or Draw — What is Driving Large-Scale Data Center Projects to Secondary Markets?", RBN Energy, GS-541) is the clearest single framing of the 2026 DC siting story that has hit the tape this week.

What "secondary market" means in this context
The "key" or primary DC markets are Northern Virginia (Loudoun/Prince William — the Ashburn cluster) and Texas (DFW and Central Texas around Austin/San Antonio). These two clusters have absorbed the bulk of hyperscale and AI-driven build-out through 2024–25. A "secondary market" is any state outside those two that is now attracting large-scale (typically 100MW+) campus projects that would historically have gone to Virginia or Texas. RBN's article specifically examines four: Indiana, Louisiana, North Carolina, and Pennsylvania.

The drivers RBN identifies
1. Power availability. Virginia's interconnection queue is effectively closed for near-term large loads; ERCOT is experiencing similar congestion around the DFW complex. Secondary markets have spare transmission headroom and, in some cases, stranded generation assets that can be re-contracted to DC load.
2. Land cost and permitting speed. Rural counties in IN/LA/NC/PA will permit a 500MW campus in months where Loudoun County is now pushing 18+ months and adding siting restrictions.
3. Utility willingness. The secondary-market utilities — Duke Energy (NC), Entergy (LA), AEP (IN), PPL (PA) — are actively bidding for DC load because it fills post-coal generation slack and provides long-tenor ratepayer stability. This is the opposite of the We Energies Wisconsin rate-case posture two days ago.
4. Tax incentive arbitrage. Louisiana's 2025 DC sales-tax exemption and Indiana's 50-year property-tax abatement window are materially more generous than Virginia's current framework.
5. Gas proximity. Louisiana (Haynesville) and Pennsylvania (Marcellus) both offer direct behind-the-meter or near-the-meter gas access for on-site generation — a differentiator as interconnection queues lengthen.

Why this matters for GroundTruth's pipeline
None of the seven deals that the classifier tagged to this signal are *named in the article itself* — the tagging reflects which GroundTruth pipeline deals have exposure to the secondary-market thesis, not a direct sponsor mention in the RBN piece. That is an important accuracy correction versus the first draft of this brief. The exposures are transmission-based:
- GT-109 SB Energy Milam — Texas gas generation with load-serving optionality; secondary-market siting shifts demand away from ERCOT DFW/Central TX toward Louisiana and the Mid-South, changing the offtaker geography for any gas generator in west/central Texas.
- Project Storybook TX / Project Temple / Project Delta Stack — Texas-sited DC-exposed projects. If primary-market congestion is the story, the *comparative* economics of Texas siting relative to IN/LA/NC/PA matter directly to underwriting assumptions.
- Project Delfin / Project Commonwealth — Louisiana Gulf Coast LNG exposure. The RBN piece's "Haynesville proximity" point is exactly the same infrastructure corridor as US LNG export; the DC and LNG stories are starting to compete for the same gas molecules and pipeline capacity in Louisiana.
- Project Temple — Texas again; same Temple/Central TX power-market dynamic as Storybook TX.

The non-obvious part
Everyone has read about DCs moving to secondary markets for 12 months. What RBN is quietly doing in this piece is naming *specific states* at the moment the primary markets start actively pushing projects away (We Energies, Maine, Loudoun restrictions). The article is the bridge between "DCs could go elsewhere" and "here is where the next 10 GW of hyperscale build is actually landing." That transition usually precedes a repricing of interconnection-queue optionality in the secondary states by 30–60 days.
